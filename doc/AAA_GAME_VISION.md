# Frontier 10052 — AAA Game Vision

## 1. Product statement

**Frontier 10052** is a cinematic hard-science-fiction spacefaring RPG and economic simulation in which the player commands an aging merchant ship at the edge of a declining human civilization.

The player trades cargo, carries news, manages a crew, maintains a fragile vessel, navigates factional law, explores old battlegrounds, and decides whether the extraordinary promise of Andromeda is salvation, propaganda, or another form of control.

The game begins in the Solar system in the year 10052. Earth is culturally sacred but economically diminished. Mars and Pluto are richer operational centers. The player gradually reaches known nearby stars, frontier regions, Gate termini, the Silent Margins, and eventually the legal route into Andromeda. Returning from Andromeda is possible, but illegal, rare, dangerous, and dependent on criminal networks.

## 2. Player fantasy

The player should feel like:

- the captain of a real, imperfect ship rather than the owner of a disposable vehicle;
- a trader whose best cargo may be information rather than goods;
- a leader responsible for people with memories, loyalties, beliefs, and private agendas;
- a small actor inside institutions much larger than themselves;
- an explorer entering places where records are incomplete and certainty is expensive;
- a person who can become wealthy, feared, trusted, hunted, exiled, or historically significant without becoming an invincible superhero.

## 3. The five creative pillars

### 3.1 Distance has weight

Warp travel takes meaningful time. Ships cannot communicate faster than light. News, prices, warrants, political changes, and personal messages travel physically.

Every departure therefore creates uncertainty. A market may change before arrival. A friend may be dead before their reply reaches you. A bounty may not yet have arrived. A captain can profit by being first, lie by being alone, or become obsolete when a faster courier docks.

### 3.2 The ship is home, tool, liability, and character

The player's vessel is old enough to have history and limited enough to require choices. Cargo space competes with fuel, spare parts, weapons, passenger accommodation, medical capacity, sensors, and smuggling compartments.

Damage should remain visible. Repairs should change the ship. Crew should name systems, remember failures, complain about modifications, and develop rituals around dangerous procedures.

### 3.3 Trust is a playable resource

Because information is delayed and records can be forged, the player constantly evaluates sources. Claims have provenance, age, corroboration, incentives, and risk.

The player asks:

- Who is the source?
- Who is the second source?
- When was the information true?
- Who benefits if I believe it?
- What happens if I arrive after everyone else?

Trust affects trade, missions, contracts, crew loyalty, insurance, legal treatment, and political access.

### 3.4 Civilization is declining unevenly

The setting is not post-apocalyptic. It is a civilization with extraordinary technology, ancient institutions, and visible fatigue.

Core systems remain wealthy but rigid. Corporate regions are efficient but extractive. Freeholds are independent but vulnerable. Syndicates provide services the law cannot. The Gate Authority is neutral, powerful, and constrained. Andromeda presents a polished democratic alternative whose hospitality has conditions.

The player should encounter competence, beauty, and kindness alongside corruption, scarcity, and decay.

### 3.5 Every solution has a social consequence

Combat is only one tool. The same problem may be solved through:

- trade;
- bribery;
- legal argument;
- reputation;
- information brokerage;
- stealth;
- forged paperwork;
- favors;
- crew expertise;
- rescue;
- intimidation;
- flight;
- combat.

The game should rarely say “wrong solution.” It should instead show who benefited, who remembers, what became harder, and what new obligation was created.

## 4. Genre and structure

The target blend is:

- space trading and route planning;
- crew-focused role-playing;
- immersive ship operations;
- systemic faction simulation;
- narrative exploration;
- tactical real-time ship encounters;
- authored cinematic story arcs;
- procedural contracts and regional events;
- economic and information speculation.

The game is primarily single-player at first. The simulation and service boundaries must preserve a future path to cooperative crews, shared markets, asynchronous player news, and selected multiplayer regions without compromising the single-player experience.

## 5. World structure

### 5.1 Starting region

The opening hours take place across the Solar system:

- Earth and its Heritage habitats: ceremonial, regulated, beautiful, economically stagnant;
- Mars: industrial, wealthy, politically connected, and essential to shipbuilding;
- Pluto Gateway: migration, freight consolidation, unofficial trade, and routes outward;
- Belt installations: fuel, repairs, labor, salvage, and old spacer culture.

### 5.2 Expanding frontier

The player unlocks routes toward real nearby stars and historically important systems. Known astronomical names should be used wherever practical. Fictional settlements, planets, stations, and local histories can be layered around real stars while preserving broad physical plausibility.

The navigable galaxy should eventually support roughly 500 meaningful stars, but production must begin with a small, dense cluster where every destination has economic and narrative identity.

### 5.3 Gates and Andromeda

Interstellar warp is the normal method of travel. Gate travel is extraordinary and extremely expensive because every transit pays a large standing activation cost plus mass-energy charges.

Migration to Andromeda is subsidized by the Andromedan government, making outward passage achievable through sponsorship, debt, service, or political qualification. Return passage is not subsidized.

Legal return is effectively unavailable to ordinary migrants. Illegal escape requires underworld contacts, rare technical access, forged clearance, and willingness to accept severe consequences. Prison can become one route into those networks.

## 6. Narrative frame

The main story should not replace the sandbox. It should provide pressure, context, and irreversible choices.

A suitable structure:

1. **Inheritance or debt** — the player gains control of an aging ship under financial and legal pressure.
2. **The first run** — a trade contract reveals that delayed information is more valuable than cargo.
3. **The frontier opens** — factions compete for the player's services and trust.
4. **The Andromeda question** — evidence challenges both Union and Federation narratives.
5. **The crossing** — the player chooses whether, how, and with whom to enter Andromeda.
6. **The cost of paradise** — the player experiences Andromeda as a functioning society with boundaries and contradictions.
7. **Stay, serve, expose, profit, or escape** — the ending emerges from accumulated relationships, evidence, debts, and choices.

## 7. Tone

The tone is mature, humane, and observant.

Avoid:

- constant grimness;
- parody as the default voice;
- omnipotent ancient aliens explaining everything;
- magical technology without cost;
- endless chosen-one escalation;
- factions that are uniformly evil or good;
- lore dumps detached from player action.

Prefer:

- competence under pressure;
- dry frontier humor;
- rituals created by dangerous work;
- beauty inside worn infrastructure;
- moral ambiguity with understandable motives;
- stories told through objects, invoices, scars, station design, overheard speech, and delayed messages.

## 8. AAA quality bar

“AAA” means each major player-facing system must meet a high bar in several dimensions at once.

### Presentation

- cinematic transitions between website, launcher, menus, and game;
- strong art direction rather than generic photorealism;
- believable scale and lighting;
- high-quality ship motion, thrusters, docking, damage, and environmental effects;
- distinctive station and faction silhouettes;
- excellent music, machinery, radio, hull, and spatial audio.

### Interaction

- responsive controls;
- readable feedback;
- minimal friction for common actions;
- layered depth for expert players;
- robust controller, mouse, keyboard, and accessibility support.

### Simulation

- prices and missions grounded in actual movement of goods and information;
- persistent consequences;
- coherent law and reputation;
- crew behavior tied to traits, history, and circumstances;
- no fake complexity that does not affect decisions.

### Narrative

- authored character arcs integrated with systemic play;
- regional voices and cultural specificity;
- consequences that reappear later;
- multiple credible perspectives;
- mysteries with evidence, not arbitrary twists.

### Engineering

- stable frame pacing;
- fast loading and streaming;
- deterministic or reproducible simulation where practical;
- strong automated testing;
- telemetry that identifies friction without manipulating the player;
- graceful operation on a range of hardware.

## 9. Anti-wiki principle

The world should feel larger than the database exposed to the player.

Not every mystery receives a codex answer. Rumors differ by save. Some wrecks, descendants, local customs, minor powers, and disputed events should be instantiated from canon-compatible templates.

The player learns through travel, relationships, records, and consequences. The codex records what the player has reason to know, including uncertainty and source provenance.

## 10. Non-negotiable setting rules

1. There is no faster-than-light communication.
2. Warp travel moves ships, not messages.
3. Warp cannot operate near strong gravity wells.
4. Gates are ancient Builder artifacts humanity can operate but cannot build or truly understand.
5. Gate travel remains expensive enough that ordinary trade still uses warp.
6. Artificial intelligence is legally and socially contested, not casually omnipotent.
7. Technology has maintenance, institutional, energy, legal, or supply-chain costs.
8. Earth begins as culturally important but no longer the richest human world.
9. Mars and Pluto are major practical centers in the opening region.
10. Andromeda is reachable, attractive, regulated, and difficult to leave.
11. The player's ship never becomes immune to economics, maintenance, law, or relationships.
12. Real stars and astronomical scale anchor the fiction wherever possible.

## 11. Success criteria

The game succeeds when players tell stories such as:

- “I made more money carrying a warning than carrying the cargo.”
- “My engineer saved us, but now she refuses to work with the security officer.”
- “I reached the station before the warrant did.”
- “I could afford the Gate only by joining a co-manifest with people I did not trust.”
- “Andromeda was better than home, and I still had to escape.”
- “The ship looked different after everything we survived.”

That is the intended identity of Frontier 10052.

# Frontier 10052 — Visual Direction

## Art statement

Cinematic hard science fiction defined by worn competence, believable scale, human adaptation, and beauty inside aging infrastructure. Avoid default military gray, sterile utopia, or universal neon cyberpunk.

## Human technology

Machines show maintenance access, heat management, structural logic, retrofit history, labels, legal seals, and supply-chain diversity. Advanced technology is impressive but never magical or costless.

## Ship language

Player-ship surfaces accumulate patches, replaced panels, scorch marks, painted warnings, personal objects, crew modifications, and faction inspection marks. Thrusters, radiators, docking hardware, cargo systems, and damage must read clearly.

## Faction identity

Differentiate factions through silhouette, materials, typography, spatial organization, lighting logic, clothing, maintenance quality, signage, and interface behavior—not only color.

## Regional identity

- Earth: ceremonial preservation, monumental memory, controlled beauty.
- Mars: industrial confidence, foundries, shipyards, engineered landscapes.
- Pluto: freight, migration, cold infrastructure, improvisation.
- Freeholds: local adaptation, reused modules, personal ownership.
- Old Battlegrounds: scale, damage, salvage, contested history.
- Silent Margins: sparse signals, uncertainty, restrained anomaly.
- Andromeda: coherent civic design, hospitality, transparency, and subtle control.

## Characters

Clothing reflects profession, environment, faction, contract status, faith, implants, and practical repair. Faces should be charismatic and lived-in rather than uniformly glamorous.

## VFX

Use realistic engine glow, maneuvering thrusters, hull reflections, dust, debris, planetary light, careful motion blur, entry plasma, weapons, defensive impacts, engine damage, jump distortion, and docking lights. Effects must communicate gameplay state.

# Frontier 10052 — Multiplayer Roadmap

## Rule

The initial game is single-player. Multiplayer must not compromise simulation depth, offline play, authored consequences, save ownership, or modding. Architecture should preserve future options without paying full MMO complexity upfront.

## Phase 1 — Social presence

Optional accounts, friends, achievements, shared screenshots, discoveries, route stories, community news, and curated statistics. No shared authoritative economy.

## Phase 2 — Asynchronous systems

Opt-in player-created market reports, courier records, community discoveries, contracts, ghost traffic, or shared regional events. Information must have provenance and moderation. Single-player saves remain independently valid.

## Phase 3 — Limited cooperation

Small cooperative crew sessions, convoys, rescue beacons, or selected synchronized regions. Sessions use server authority for shared state while preserving local presentation and accessibility settings.

## Shared economy

A global market is high risk because automation, cheating, time zones, scale, and speculation can destroy the information-delay design. Begin with bounded regional or seasonal experiments and strong sinks.

## Architecture

Stable entity IDs, deterministic simulation boundaries, command validation, event logs, versioned protocols, account separation, entitlement services, moderation, rate limits, observability, and migration strategy.

## Safety and operations

Reporting, blocking, privacy, age-appropriate community controls, anti-harassment tools, anti-cheat, incident response, service status, and graceful offline fallback are mandatory before real-time multiplayer.

## Go/no-go criteria

Proceed only after the single-player core is excellent, audience demand is measured, synchronization prototypes succeed, operating costs are understood, and multiplayer adds stories unavailable in solo play. A persistent-world MMO is outside the current roadmap.

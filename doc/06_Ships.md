# Frontier 10052 — Ships

## Philosophy

The player ship is home, tool, liability, workplace, collateral, and character. It begins old, useful, indebted, and visibly imperfect. Damage remains visible; repairs and modifications change appearance, sound, performance, and crew behavior.

## Core systems

- hull, pressure, armor, and structural integrity;
- metric drive and pinch lattice;
- conventional engines and maneuvering thrusters;
- power generation, storage, and distribution;
- thermal control and radiators;
- sensors, communications, navigation, and identity modules;
- cargo handling and storage;
- life support, medical bay, quarters, and airlocks;
- weapons, countermeasures, and defensive fields where canon permits;
- docking and utility systems.

## Configuration

Mass, volume, power, heat, hardpoints, crew workload, legal class, and maintenance burden constrain builds. Cargo space competes with fuel, spares, weapons, passenger accommodation, medical capacity, sensors, and smuggling compartments.

## Wear and damage

Systems accumulate wear from use, age, heat, overload, poor parts, impacts, and neglected service. Damage propagates through dependencies: a radiator failure limits power, weak power reduces sensors, poor sensors increase ambush risk.

## Repair

Repairs consume time, parts, expertise, money, dock access, and sometimes favors. Field repairs restore function with penalties; shipyard work restores or upgrades capability. Crew remember dangerous improvisations and may name unreliable systems.

## Ownership

The ship can carry debt, liens, disputed title, insurance requirements, inspection history, and inherited obligations. Selling or replacing it is possible but narratively and economically significant.

## Progression

Upgrades create specialization and reliability, not universal superiority. Every configuration has trade-offs and remains subject to mass, heat, law, maintenance, and crew competence.
